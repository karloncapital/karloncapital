There's a moment in Annie Dillard's *Pilgrim at Tinker Creek* where she watches a frog being consumed from the inside by a giant water bug — the frog's body collapsing like a deflating balloon while its eyes remain wide open. Most people would look away. Dillard keeps watching. That is the whole of her practice, really: the refusal to look away.

We speak of attention as though it were a resource we can budget — a fixed sum to be spent wisely between work and family and the thousand daily demands that press upon us. But attention is more like a muscle, and most of us have never been taught to use it. We've been taught to manage it, to protect it from distraction, to ration it carefully. We have not been taught to simply aim it at the world and hold it there.

> The secret of seeing is to sail on solar wind. Hone and spread your spirit till you yourself are a sail, whetted, translucent, broadside to the merest puff.

## The Cost of Half-Looking

A few weeks ago I spent an hour at the lake with a fishing rod and no real expectation of catching anything. The rod was almost beside the point. What I noticed, for the first time in months, was the quality of the light on the water at that particular hour — the way it fractured and moved, each fragment distinct, collectively forming something that couldn't quite be described as a surface anymore.

I had been to that lake dozens of times. I had never seen that before. Or rather: I had never looked long enough for it to become visible.

This is what half-looking costs us. Not the dramatic thing — not wisdom or enlightenment or a rich inner life, though perhaps those too. What it costs, most practically, is contact with the actual texture of the world we live in. The specific quality of a Tuesday afternoon in June. The way certain conversations have a weight that persists after the words are gone. These things don't announce themselves. They have to be noticed.

## A Practice, Not a Theory

I don't think the solution is to consume another essay about the importance of mindfulness, or to download an app, or to block out a sacred hour for contemplation. These approaches tend to add more structure to a life that is already over-structured.

What I've found more useful is something simpler and harder: the discipline of staying with one thing until it becomes interesting. It almost always does. The trick is in the staying.
